Internalized Orientalism in Koera

Forest City boasts luxurious condos with sea views. Views to be enjoyed from afar, for if you walk along the seafront you will be met with warning signs that read “NO SWIMMING”. So much for going to the beach. But as it turns out––no one is really planning to.


The beach in Forest City, with Singapore's shoreline in the distance.


The urban mega development, consisting of a concrete superstructure covering four newly created islands, at the southern tip of Malaysia in the Johor Strait, spans over 1700 hectares, equivalent to about half the size of Central Park. It is a private gated property built by one of the largest property development companies in China, Country Garden Pacific View.

The development is strategically located close to Singapore.


18 movies were produced from 2010 through 2019 with Korean Chinese appearing, out of which Chinese Koreans were portrayed as villains, hit-men, traffickers, gangsters, thieves, and undocumented aliens in 13 movies.

In the rest 5 movies, they were depicted as "normal" people or in a more diverse way. "Hide and Seek" is an example.

Condo towers are scattered across the island. Draped in greenery, they dot the city skyline––yet remain empty. Many choose to purchase property in Forest City, but for most it will not even serve as a landing pad. The current occupancy rates are low: those who have bought in have been enticed by the prospective returns on their investment rather than the prospect of living there.



Towers of luxury condos on the main island, most of which remain empty.


The liveliest place in town is the Forest City showroom on the main island: a large open space with mock-up condos, created so that you can almost imagine yourself living there. They are complete with scribbled post-it notes on unplugged fridges and models posing as family in framed photos. The showroom is busy with sales representatives, and prospective buyers, most of whom are visitors from China. A large gong sits in the corner and is hit in celebration each time a condo sale is closed—the only live music to be heard on the island. haha


The developers advertise promises of a smart and green city which resolves many ills of urban life, from pollution to traffic congestion. Even an underground transportation network is said to be planned, leaving room for pedestrians above ground.


Although economically enticing, this project presents significant environmental and social cost. Swimming at the beach, as tempting as it sounds, is not recommended because of the risk of crocodiles roaming. Their habitat was disrupted to make way for the condos; before swaths of sand were posed in the area, it was covered in dense mangroves home to a rich variety of wildlife. Being built entirely from scratch, it is a resource-intensive endeavor. Its construction is dependent on migrant laborers who live in shared housing units on the mainland, away from the city.


Despite its uncertain success, Forest City is one of several new urban developments in Malaysia aimed at boosting the regional economy; others include Puteri Harbour and the Melaka Gateway. This forms part of a national economic strategy to rebrand the country to foreign investors and leverage the country’s proximity to Singapore.


Walking down the main commercial street in the evening after 8pm, there are no open shops or restaurants to be found. The only option is the restaurant at the only hotel. No life in the buildings—or between them, for that matter. One can only wonder why anyone wanted them there in the first place.
