Korean Chinese


By Seulgi Jung


South Korea is one of the countries in which reading news via online channels is the most common way and, of course, has its own unique way of online news consumption. Search engine companies like Naver, the country's biggest search engine, run its own online news platform by curating articles provided from a variety of sources alongside open comment sections. The corporates neglect the comment sections, easily plagued by inappropriate and hateful remarks. Cut to the chase, I recently found some interesting phenomena in those remarks, commenters of which accuse one another of “Joseonjok" — a term coined by Joseon the last dynasty of South Korea and Jok meaning ethnic group — saying they are attempting to overturn the public opinion when other’s opinions are not in line with theirs.


Defined as Chinese of Korean descent who are regarded as one of the 50 minor ethnic tribes, It is technically not a derogatory name. Before there was Joseonjok, they were called “Dongpo", the literal definition of which is siblings who were born to the same mother, widely used to call Koreans living overseas. It has been replaced with Joseonjok because of immensely increasing antipathy against inclusivity within the term and China over the years. The chart is a clear example that shows the surge in usage volume of the term since 2020.

As you might have noticed from the chart, it was not only about South Korea. Pew Research Center said that distasteful views of China had reached its unprecedented peak in the wake of pandemic in many countries for supposedly mishandling with COVID-19.

But views have continued to sour and today, and unfavorable views of China are at a historic high of 80% according to 2022 research by the institution. South Koreans answered China’s involvement in politics in their country as the top concern while China’s policies on human rights were described as a very serious problem more often than other issues in other countries.

Does the Chinese government interfere with South Korea’s politics? Why does this hatred flow down to Chinese expatriates we once believed to share a uniform ethnic identity then? But there might be two more possible reasons for it.


China Gate and Never Verified Claims

It was March 2020, when harsh feelings against China were soaring for 2019 novel coronavirus, one anonymous user in Ilbe, a notoriously far right anything-goes forum, claimed that they are part of Chinese Koreans ordered to do online operations of dividing the public by writing pro-China posts and comments, which was quickly picked up by traditionally conservative news media and made headlines.

The internet companies denied the accusation and released records of Chinese IP being accounted for only 0.5%. Former United Future Party, current ruling party renamed People Power Party, stood firm and went further with the next step by filing a criminal complaint in March 2020, the result of which has not been shared yet until 3 years later at this moment.

First Visible Immigrants in An Intensely Exclusive Society

For decades, immigrants had been nearly invisible to Koreans. They were thought to only exist in factories and farm fields in rural areas, taking dangerous low-waged jobs. Korean Chinese broke the tacit rule, flooding and blending into a long-forbidden space since the Act on the Immigration and Legal Status of Overseas Korean was introduced. They have a huge advantage, being fluent in Korean, widely hailed by mid-to-small sized companies and industries suffering shortage of labor force.

Many scholars stated that the mechanism of biases and discrimination works differently here in Korea: It was often pointed out that Koreans adopted lenses of orientalism in the beginning through which perceiving the world and deeply internalized materialism played a big role too. But they also say that the reason Korean Chinese are especially targeted is because they make up the largest number of immigrants, being the most visible outsiders experiencing racism barely surfaced or even addressed in the closed society before any other groups of immigrants do.

How media promoted hatred against Joseonjok

From 2010 through 2019, South Korean culture had a renaissance. The country started to emerge as a culture house in the early 2000s, not only producing creative k-pop music and original shows but also well-regarded films. In particular, for the film industry, the 2010 era seemed to decorate its finale with international awards and prizes when Parasite, a satire movie about capitalism by Bong Joon-Ho, swept the Canne and Oscar.

Presenting itself with the very high quality and maturity, the industry was blamed for insensitive portrayal of immigrants at the same time. In 2017, Josenjok immigrants group made a collective statement to plead suspension of screening of 청년경찰 in theaters, saying it fueled hatred toward Joseonjok and aroused unnecessary tension between Koreans and Chinese. In the movie, 대림-dong where predominantly Joseonjok resided was described as such a town plagued with violent crime that even police can’t dare to control.

18 movies were produced in the 2010s with Korean Chinese characters appearing, out of which 13 works portrayed them as villains, hit-men, traffickers, gangsters, thieves, and undocumented aliens.

The other 5 movies depicted Joseonjok as "normal" people or in a more diverse way, but not in a way of depicting them as friendly ordinary neighbors.

Actually the perception that Joseonjok is highly involved with crimes does not align with reality. Published by the National Police Agency, the 2022 Crime Statistics shows that chinese crime rate is 1.2%, even if narrowed down to violent crime, it’s 1.17. It’s hard to conclude that the number of crimes committed by Chinese is distinctly high given that 1.6% of the population is Chinese.

“In fact, in society, minorities are often caught up in bad things. Even in Korea, people from that side of the world often commit violent crimes, which is why Korean movies often feature such settings,” Kwak Kyung-taek, director of Resurrected Victims where a Joseonjok character was depicted as a killer, answered a question about a controversial issue of them making an appearance as villains again in an interview in 2017. “As a storyteller, I think I had no choice. I couldn’t give up on things like Chinatown as a fictional space in that sense. A movie has to be plausible, and if you think about these things too much, you can’t do that. If I have the opportunity to tell their story someday, I want to do it right, and I’ll make up for it then,” he added.
